Automate data generation

Prediction systems can support or substitute human labor. Deploying successful data pipelines requires considering and designing for the people who will use the system.

The Luddites asked for a voice in automation

We need to think about who contributes to, is impacted by, and benefits from automation. As an example, the Luddite movement began by mill workers being disenfranchised by imposed automation. A start to ensuring alignment is to ask people what they want. From there, we can co-create solutions that solve stakeholder problems.

So, I work to ensure that the people involved in data work are a part of the solution.

Assess model predictions

All models are wrong. They are reduced representations of the world that allow us to predict the future.

This is not a pipe

There is a logical fallacy, highlighted by philosopher Alfred Korzybski, of mistaking the map for the territory. Since the map is not the territory, predictions from the model will never be a 1:1 representation of the truth. A way around this fallacy is to understand where the model is wrong.

So, I work to measure the inaccuracy of the predictions that come from data pipelines and models that I build.

Example: I‚Äôve continuously monitored predictive errors from a machine learning model that I built to predict flight times between any two places in the US at flyExclusive.
